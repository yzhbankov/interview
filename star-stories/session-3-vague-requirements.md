# Handling Vague Requirements

## STAR Framework Reference

- **S** - Situation (2 sentences)
- **T** - Task (1-2 sentences)
- **A** - Action (4-5 sentences)
- **R** - Result (2-3 sentences)

---

## 1. Handling Vague Requirements

**Situation:** A Product Owner initiated an idea to build a QA Platform intended to partially replace the QA department, which had recently been laid off. The goal was to introduce an AI-driven solution, but the concept was still immature and positioned as an MVP, with very high-level requirements and no clear functional or non-functional specifications.

**Task:** I was assigned as the system architect and project owner. My responsibility was to translate the vague vision into a concrete technical design and delivery plan.

**Action:** To handle the ambiguity, I first organized a series of structured discovery sessions with the Product Owner to clarify the product vision and expected outcomes. I also involved senior engineers early in the process to challenge assumptions and identify missing requirements from both functional and non-functional perspectives (such as scalability, reliability, and test coverage expectations). Through iterative discussions, I facilitated alignment between product and engineering, gradually transforming the high-level idea into a well-defined requirements set. Once the scope became clear and validated, I led the team in designing the system architecture and delivery plan based on these agreed requirements.

**Result:** We successfully delivered the QA Platform aligned with the Product Owner's vision. The project was completed ahead of schedule due to reduced ambiguity early in the process, and the team had a clear and stable design foundation that minimized rework during implementation.
